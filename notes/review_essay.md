# The Economics of an Emergency Room Visit: Three Decades of Valuing Air Pollution's Health Costs

When the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) estimates the benefits of a proposed air quality regulation, it must answer a deceptively simple question: how much is an avoided emergency room visit worth? In the current version of BenMAP-CE, the agency's primary benefits-assessment tool, the answer for a cardiovascular ER visit is $1,161 in 2015 dollars (EPA, 2025). That figure is the endpoint of a methodological lineage stretching back to the late 1980s --- one shaped by competing valuation philosophies, data constraints, and institutional choices that continue to define the field today.

At the center of this history is a tension between two approaches. **Cost-of-Illness (COI)** adds up the tangible costs of a health event: medical bills and, in some cases, lost wages. It is concrete, replicable, and grounded in administrative data. **Willingness-to-Pay (WTP)**, by contrast, asks how much a person would pay to avoid the entire experience --- not just the bills, but the pain, anxiety, and disruption to daily life. WTP is widely regarded by economists as the theoretically correct welfare measure because it captures everything a health event does to someone, not just what shows up on an invoice (U.S. EPA, 2010). Yet for ER visits related to air pollution, EPA has used COI for three decades and continues to do so. This essay traces the development of that choice through three chronological phases: a foundation-laying period in the 1990s when the first COI estimates emerged, a period of challenge from 2000 to 2006 when WTP research matured and quantified the gap, and a period of institutional settlement from 2004 to the present in which EPA acknowledged the gap but entrenched COI through infrastructure investments and advisory endorsements. The resulting methodology is a pragmatic compromise whose limitations are well-documented but unresolved.

## The First Cost Estimates: Laying the COI Foundation (1990s)

The earliest ER visit valuations available to EPA analysts were cost-of-illness estimates drawn from small-scale survey and hospital datasets, and their methodological differences foreshadowed a data-quality debate the agency would spend the next two decades managing.

In 1997, Smith and colleagues published a national estimate of asthma-related healthcare costs using data from a 1987 household survey. They identified approximately 1.2 million asthma ER visits at a total cost of roughly $186.5 million, yielding an average of about $155 per visit in 1987 dollars --- equivalent to $534 when adjusted for medical inflation to 2015 dollars (Smith et al., 1997). This was a **total-expenditure** approach: the figure reflected what households reported paying or being billed, without distinguishing between the hospital's actual cost of delivering care and the markup embedded in the bill.

Two years later, Stanford and colleagues took a different tack. Using internal cost-accounting records from a multi-hospital system for 1996--1997, they estimated the average cost of an asthma ER visit at approximately $235 in 1997 dollars, or $447 in 2015 dollars (Stanford et al., 1999). Crucially, this was an **actual-resource-consumption** approach: because the researchers had access to the hospital's own accounting of what care actually cost to deliver, their figure stripped away the billing markup that inflated Smith's estimate.

The resulting divergence --- $534 versus $447 in comparable 2015 dollars --- may appear modest, but it reflects a fundamental methodological split between charges and costs that persists in health economics to this day. Smith's higher figure includes hospital markup; Stanford's lower figure excludes it. Neither study extended beyond asthma to cardiovascular or general respiratory ER visits, leaving those endpoints entirely unvalued. Both limitations --- the charges-versus-costs ambiguity and the narrow disease scope --- would take years to resolve. In the meantime, both estimates entered the regulatory toolkit and, as we will see, remain embedded in BenMAP-CE nearly three decades later.

## The WTP Challenge: Quantifying What COI Misses (2000--2006)

While the COI literature was establishing baseline cost figures, a parallel strand of health economics research was asking a fundamentally different question: what would people actually pay to avoid an air-pollution-related health event? Between 2000 and 2006, a cluster of willingness-to-pay studies demonstrated that COI substantially understates the welfare loss from acute respiratory and cardiac illness, creating a theoretical challenge to any valuation framework built on medical costs alone.

The most direct early comparison came from Alberini and Krupnick (2000), who conducted a contingent valuation study in Taiwan. Over 900 residents kept daily health diaries, and when someone fell ill, researchers asked how much they would pay to avoid a recurrence. Comparing WTP bids against the corresponding cost-of-illness for the same episodes, they found that willingness-to-pay was approximately **two to four times larger** than COI for acute respiratory illness (Alberini & Krupnick, 2000). The gap represented everything a medical bill cannot capture: pain, disrupted plans, anxiety, and lost leisure time. Although the Taiwan setting raises questions about whether the ratio transfers directly to the United States --- income levels, healthcare systems, and cultural attitudes toward health spending all differ --- the direction and magnitude of the finding proved remarkably consistent with later research.

That same year, Johnson, Banzhaf, and Desvousges (2000) made a primarily methodological contribution. They developed a multiple-format stated-preference approach in which survey participants evaluated health states described using a modified version of the Quality of Well-Being (QWB) Index, scoring symptoms, duration, activity restrictions, and cost. By combining two different question formats --- graded-pair comparisons and discrete-choice experiments --- they demonstrated that contingent valuation could produce internally valid WTP estimates even for short-duration acute health events (Johnson et al., 2000). This was significant because a standing objection to WTP for ER-type endpoints was that survey respondents could not meaningfully value brief, non-fatal episodes. Johnson and colleagues showed they could, expanding the feasibility frontier for WTP as a practical alternative to COI.

Building on this methodological groundwork, Stieb and colleagues (2002) produced the study that most directly challenged BenMAP's COI framework. Working with Canadian data, they combined cost-of-illness estimates with WTP data informed by stated-preference methods to produce comprehensive valuations for air-pollution-related health events. Crucially, their estimates covered ER visits as a distinct endpoint: CAN$4,400 per cardiac ED visit (95% CI CAN$3,300--$5,600) and CAN$2,000 per respiratory ED visit (95% CI CAN$1,700--$2,500), both in 1997 Canadian dollars (Stieb et al., 2002). Converting these to 2015 U.S. dollars (multiplying by the 1997 CAD/USD exchange rate of 0.72 and the CPI-Medical inflation factor of approximately 1.90) yields roughly $5,700 for a cardiac ED visit and $2,700 for a respiratory ED visit. Compared to BenMAP's COI-only values of $1,161 and $875 for the same endpoints, the comprehensive WTP-inclusive estimates are approximately **five times** and **three times** larger, respectively. The pattern was now clear across multiple disease categories, countries, and study designs: COI was not merely a conservative estimate but a distant lower bound.

The culmination of this period came with Van Houtven, Powers, Jessup, and Yang (2006), who synthesized over 230 WTP estimates drawn from 17 published studies of acute health symptoms into a meta-regression. Rather than examining one condition at a time, they asked what systematically predicts how much people are willing to pay to avoid a health event. The strongest predictors were illness severity (as measured by QWB scores) and episode duration, followed by respondent income and study methodology (Van Houtven et al., 2006). More consequentially for policy, their regression coefficients could serve as **benefit-transfer functions** --- formulas that estimate WTP for health conditions that have never been directly studied, by plugging in a condition's severity and duration characteristics. This represented a potential off-ramp from the COI approach: if benefit-transfer functions could generate defensible WTP values for BenMAP's specific ER endpoints, the agency would no longer need to wait for primary WTP studies of each individual diagnosis category. However, the meta-regression also revealed substantial heterogeneity across the underlying studies, introducing a layer of transfer uncertainty that regulatory analysts would ultimately prove reluctant to accept.

By 2006, the empirical landscape was well-mapped. Comprehensive WTP-inclusive valuations exceeded COI by roughly three times for respiratory ED visits and five times for cardiac ED visits. The ratio was not constant but scaled with severity --- the more frightening and life-threatening the health event, the larger the share of total welfare loss that COI failed to capture. The question was no longer whether the gap existed but whether EPA would act on it.

## Institutional Settlement: Choosing COI in Practice (2004--2025)

Despite the growing body of evidence that COI substantially understates the welfare loss from ER visits, EPA moved in the opposite direction from what the WTP literature might have predicted. Through a series of advisory endorsements, formal guidelines, and data infrastructure investments, the agency institutionalized COI as its operational standard while formally acknowledging WTP as the theoretical ideal.

The first institutional marker was the EPA Science Advisory Board's 2004 advisory (EPA-SAB-COUNCIL-ADV-04-002), which endorsed the use of COI for ER visit endpoints. A follow-up advisory in 2009 (EPA-SAB-09-012) reaffirmed this position. The SAB did not reject WTP on theoretical grounds; rather, it pointed to the practical difficulties of applying available WTP estimates --- derived from different countries, different health endpoints, and different survey instruments --- to the specific ER visit categories used in EPA's benefits analyses. The SAB's endorsement provided institutional cover for continued COI use and established a precedent that would prove difficult to reverse.

In 2010, EPA's own *Guidelines for Preparing Economic Analyses* codified the compromise. The Guidelines state explicitly that WTP is the **preferred welfare measure** for valuing changes in health risk and that COI provides only a lower-bound estimate because it omits pain, suffering, lost leisure, and averting behavior (U.S. EPA, 2010). Yet the same document permits COI when "reliable" WTP estimates are unavailable for the specific endpoint in question. For ER visits, EPA concluded that no existing WTP study was specific enough --- nobody had surveyed U.S. residents about their willingness to pay to avoid a cardiovascular ER visit coded to ICD-9 390--459, or a respiratory ER visit coded to ICD-9 460--519. The "lower bound" framing is analytically important: by characterizing COI this way, EPA implicitly acknowledges that every dollar figure in BenMAP's ER visit calculations is a deliberate undercount. A regulation that passes a cost-benefit test using these conservative numbers would only look better under WTP.

The most consequential development, however, may not have been any single policy statement but rather EPA's investment in the data infrastructure underlying BenMAP-CE. As documented in Appendix H of the current User's Manual (EPA, 2025), the agency now draws ER visit costs from the Healthcare Cost and Utilization Project (HCUP), specifically the Nationwide Emergency Department Sample (NEDS) and the Nationwide Inpatient Sample (NIS). The NEDS alone contains approximately 30 million ER visit records from roughly 950 hospitals. The BenMAP pipeline filters these records by diagnosis code (bridging ICD-9 to ICD-10 via a published crosswalk), converts billed charges to estimated real costs using cost-to-charge ratios borrowed from the NIS, computes weighted national averages that account for the sampling design, and runs Monte Carlo simulations to characterize uncertainty. The resulting unit values --- $1,161 for cardiovascular, $875 for general respiratory, $11,990 for emergency hospital admissions among those 65 and older --- are grounded in a massive, continuously updated administrative dataset with transparent methodology (EPA, 2025).

Yet alongside this modern infrastructure, BenMAP still offers the Smith and Stanford asthma values --- $534 and $447 --- as alternatives. These figures, derived from data collected in 1987 and 1996--1997 respectively, have survived every update cycle. Their persistence illustrates how foundational estimates become embedded in regulatory infrastructure: they have been used in major rulemakings, peer-reviewed extensively, and relied upon for cross-regulation comparisons. Replacing them would complicate the comparison of benefits across decades of regulatory actions.

The HCUP investment also made COI self-reinforcing in a subtler way. The database provides exactly what a COI calculation needs --- diagnosis codes, charges, cost-to-charge ratios, and sampling weights --- and nothing that a WTP calculation would require, such as preference data or quality-of-life scores. Each improvement to the HCUP pipeline further reduced the marginal cost of maintaining COI estimates while leaving the cost of developing comparable WTP estimates unchanged. The infrastructure, in other words, created its own inertia.

## Conclusion

The history of ER visit valuation for air pollution benefits analysis reveals a consistent pattern: a well-established empirical finding --- that comprehensive WTP-inclusive valuations exceed cost-of-illness by a factor of roughly three to five for ER visits --- that has not been translated into regulatory practice. This is not because the finding is disputed. The WTP literature, synthesized most comprehensively by Van Houtven et al. (2006), shows broad agreement that COI captures at best one-quarter to one-half of the total welfare loss from a pollution-related ER visit. The gap grows with the severity of the health event, from a roughly threefold multiple for respiratory ED visits to a roughly fivefold multiple for cardiac ED visits.

The most consequential moment in this timeline was arguably not any single study but EPA's investment in a scalable, updateable COI pipeline built on HCUP administrative data. By making COI easy to compute and hard to replace, this infrastructure transformed a temporary practical choice into a durable institutional commitment. The SAB endorsements and the Guidelines' "lower bound" framing provided the policy rationale, but the data infrastructure provided the operational lock-in.

Whether the agency will eventually adopt WTP-based or WTP-adjusted values for ER endpoints remains an open question. The benefit-transfer approach developed by Van Houtven et al. (2006) offers one potential path: rather than waiting for primary WTP studies of each specific endpoint, the agency could use severity-based transfer functions to estimate WTP from existing research. The heterogeneity that currently limits this approach may prove addressable as the underlying literature grows. Until then, every BenMAP estimate of avoided ER visit costs carries an implicit asterisk: the true benefit to the people who did not have to make that trip to the emergency room is almost certainly several times larger than the number on the page.
